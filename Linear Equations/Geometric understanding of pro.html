Geometric understanding of properties of proportions

Consider two lines CA and CB. Let CA be divided into equal number of parts  \(ce = er = rs = ...\)   and let \(eh , rm , sp\) … be drawn parallel to AB, then CB will be divided into same number of equal parts \(ch = hm = mp = ...\)

     

Draw hn, mo, ... parallel to CA ; then the triangles ceh and hnm will be similar ; because of the parallel lines, ehnr will be a parallelogram , and therefore nh = re = ec, Lmhnhce, and hnm = ceh ;  , hm = ch. In precisely the same way it may be shown that mp = ch, and so on.

Taking any of the points r in CA, it follows that cr will be contained in CA as many times as cm is contained in CB, that is

\(\frac {CA}{Cr} = \frac {CB}{Cm}\)  or \(CA:Cr :: CB:Cm\)

This derivation is known as Basic Proportionality theorem, derived in NCERT Class X, Theorem 6.1, using a different geometric principle.  

Derivation of other properties

Using the same above approach, we will notice that 

\(\frac{Cr}{Ce} = \frac{Cm}{Ch}\)   which is \(\frac {Ce+er}{Ce} = \frac{Cm+hm}{Cm}\)  on simplification gives \(1+\frac{er}{Ce} = 1 + \frac{hm}{Cm}\)  which gives the relation \(\frac{er}{Ce} = \frac{hm}{Cm}\) ( Which is true by the geometric reasoning above).

All other properties can be derived similarly.

Observations from the construction :

When two lines intersect, a similar triangle and a parallelogram are form.

If the two lines were not intersecting, say represent the same line (overlapping), neither the triangle nor the parallelogram will be formed.

If the two lines were parallel, only the parallelograms would have formed, the similar triangles will not be formed.

These observations will be later used in solutions of linear equations. 

What if the line CA cannot be equally divided using Cr as a measure ?

This is a typical challenge that the student will face doing this construction activity.  This is an opportunity to learn about Limits, without actually needing to know the term “limits”.

Let cr be divided into any number of equal parts, and measure off one of them from r towards A ; let an be the portion in excess, then draw nv parallel to AB. Because cn and cr are commensurable, we have, by the above theorem,  \(\frac {cm}{cr} = \frac {cv}{cn}\) ;

but \(Cn= CA + An\) and  \(CV = CB + Bv\), by substitution

\(\frac {CA+An}{Cr} = \frac {CB+Bv}{Cm}\)  or \(\frac {CA}{Cr} + \frac{An}{Cr} = \frac{Cb}{Cm} + \frac{Bv}{Cm}\)

By increasing the number of divisions in cr, the excess An and вv may BU be made as small as we please; therefore \(\frac{An}{Cr}\) and  \(\frac{Bv}{Cm}\) may be indefinitely decreased ( to the extent we want) and will ultimately have \(\frac {CA}{Cr} = \frac {CB}{Cm}\)
