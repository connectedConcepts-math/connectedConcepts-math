Solving Linear Equations as Equivalent Fractions

Using Equivalent fractions : Linear equations that are formed as ratios will be converted to equivalent fractions and solved using ‚Äú Visual Fractions‚Äù ( Like the whole-part diagrams that are introduced in Class 4/5.  The same problem is solved without the need for visuals.

Example 1

Ratio of John's age to his Father 10 years ago is 1:5. Six years later, the ratio of their ages becomes 3:7.  Find their current ages.

 

Visual approach

The idea here is to make bars representing the two quantities, showing the ‚Äúparts‚Äù used in each ratio without knowing their actual size, and relate them to one another. 

In the particular problem, the ratios of the ages were such that the difference in ratio was 4 (constant) . Hence the bars representing the ratios could be aligned easily. 

Next is an example, where the difference in ratios are not the same.

Algebraic formulation 

Let J be John's age, and F be Father's age.

\((J-10):(F-10) = 1:5\)   [ 10 years ago]

\((J+6):(F+6) = 3:7\)  [ 6 years later ]

Using Proportions :

\((J+6):)J-10)::3:1\)

\(16:(J-10)::4:1\) ( Using Componendo)

\(15:(J-10)::16:4\)     or  \((J-10)=4  \space J=14\)

Similarly,

\((F+6):(F-10) :: 7:5\)

\(16L(F-10) :: 2:5\)

\(16L(F-10) :: 15:30 or F=50\)

Today, the ratio of John‚Äôs age to his father‚Äôs is 1:6. In 6 years, it will be 1:3. How old are they?

Notice that the difference in ratio of ages of Father and John, in first case (Current) is 6-1 = 5. The difference in ages of Father and Jon, 6 years later, is 3-1 = 2. As a result of difference in these ages not being same, the bars representing them will not align, as seen in the below :

The bars don‚Äôt have the same scale in each ratio, as we see from the fact that the difference of ages is not the same; so we can‚Äôt just add years. We need to rescale the diagrams so that the difference is the same size in each.

To make them line up, we need to make equivalent ratios such that the differences, rather than being 5 and 2, will both be 10 (the LCM). So I multiply by 2 and 5, respectively, rewriting \(1:6\) as  \(2:12\), and \(1:3\) as  \(5:15\). The new bars do line up correctly

Clearly 3 units makes 6 years, so each unit is 2 years. so John was 4 and his father was 24. (In 6 years, they will be 10 and 30, with the required ratio.)

Observation from these examples

In both the examples, the algebraic formulation was of the type 

\((x-a) : (y-a) :: m:n\)  [ a years before ]

\((x-b):(y-b) :: p:q\)  [ b years before ]

In both the equations, the number subtracted ( could be added, depending on context) in both numerator and denominator are the same. 

Approach could be used when \((m-n)=(p-q)\). When \((m-n) \neq (p-q)\), then equivalent fractions have to be established on right hand side.

The  approach begets the question, Can this approach be used to solve pair of linear equations that are NOT expressed as ratios, that is the equations are expressed in the form of \(ax+by=r\) and  \(cx+dy =s\) ? The next examples will cover conversion of equations of this form to ratio form and then solve them.
